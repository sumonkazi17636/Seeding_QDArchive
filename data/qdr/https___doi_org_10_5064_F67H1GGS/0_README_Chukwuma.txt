While 79 percent of Nigerian mothers who deliver in facilities receive postnatal care within 48 hours of delivery, only 16 percent of mothers who deliver outside facilities receive postnatal care within this period.  Most maternal deaths can be prevented with access to timely and competent health care.  Thus, the World Health Organization, International Confederation of Midwives, and International Federation of Gynecology and Obstetrics recommend that unskilled birth attendants be involved in advocacy for skilled care use among mothers. This study explores postnatal care referral behavior by TBAs (Traditional Birth Attendants) in Nigeria, including the perceived factors that may deter or promote referrals to skilled health workers.  The study was conducted in July 2016 in Ebonyi State, South-Eastern Nigeria, where about 1 in 2 mothers does not receive postnatal care within the first two days of childbirth.  We collected qualitative data using focus group discussions (FGD) involving 28 female health workers, TBAs, and TBA delivery clients.  We held one FGD with 8 health workers, another with 10 TBAs, and a final FGD with 10 TBA delivery clients (9 of whom came for the discussion with their newborns).  All the participants were female.  As part of a larger mixed methods study, the study team purposively selected 128 wards in Ebonyi State that had at least one primary health care facility with a health care provider offering maternal postnatal care. We identified these wards using the national facility census list and via consultations with officials in the State Ministry of Health.  The topic guides for this study were developed in line with the study conceptual framework, which drew on constructs in Fishbein and AjzenÆs theory of reasoned action, to which we mapped hypothesized determinants of postnatal care referrals described in the empirical literature. We found that differences in TBA referral before, during, and after delivery appear to reflect the TBAÆs understanding of the added value of skilled care for the client and the TBA, as well as the TBAÆs perception of the implications of referral for her credibility as a maternal care provider among her clients.
